Term 1 of 5
Hour Bank
A construction union member works 200 hours in January — well above her plan's threshold. In February, the sites go quiet and she only works 40 hours. Her plan requires 150 hours per month to be eligible.

She stays covered in March even though February was a short month. Why?

✓ Correct — that's what the bank is for

Hour Bank eligibility is month-to-month — but balances carry forward. When a member works more hours than the plan requires, the surplus goes into their bank. When a quiet month comes, they draw on that balance to stay covered.

This is the fundamental difference from group billing, where coverage is continuous by enrolment. In Hour Bank plans, coverage has to be earned each cycle — the bank just smooths out the gaps. Once the bank is empty, coverage stops unless the member self-pays or a subsidy applies.

✗ Group billing works differently

Group billing is continuous — enrolled means covered, and coverage doesn't depend on hours. Hour Bank is not continuous. Eligibility must be earned each month through hours worked. The reason she stays covered after a short month is that surplus hours from busier months are banked and drawn on when needed.

✗ There's no automatic grace period — there's a bank

Hour Bank plans don't grant grace periods. What they do is allow members to bank surplus hours from months when they work above the threshold. In January she worked 200 hours against a 150-hour requirement — those 50 surplus hours went into her bank. February's 40 hours weren't enough on their own, but the banked hours made up the difference.

Eligibility Month
Ellie works 160 hours in January. At the end of January, Oliver sends her hours to the insurance company. The insurer processes them during February. Her coverage is confirmed for March.

In Multiplan, March is called her Eligibility Month. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — and the two-month gap is the part worth remembering

Eligibility Month is the coverage month — the outcome at the end of a three-step chain:

✗ That's the work month, not the eligibility month

January is the work month — when hours are earned. The Eligibility Month comes two steps later, after employer submission and insurer processing. Work in January → submitted in February → eligible in March. March is the Eligibility Month.

Dollar Bank
Two members, same union, same trust fund. Member A's plan tracks the hours she works each month — work enough hours, stay covered. Member B's plan works differently: his employer pays $2.30 per hour worked into a running balance. When that balance reaches the plan's dollar threshold, he's eligible.

Member B is on a Dollar Bank plan. What's the key difference from Member A's Hour Bank?

✓ Correct — same engine, different unit

Hour Bank and Dollar Bank share exactly the same logic: eligibility is earned month to month, balances carry forward, and a threshold must be met. The only difference is what gets counted — hours worked versus dollars contributed (typically hours × an agreed contribution rate).

Both plan types can exist within the same union trust fund for different member categories. Multiplan handles both in the same platform, which is part of what makes it suited to complex union environments.

✗ Dollar Bank is not continuous

Continuous eligibility is how group billing works — enrolled means covered. Dollar Bank is month-to-month, just like Hour Bank. The employer contributes dollars based on hours worked each period. If those dollars don't meet the plan's threshold, Member B isn't eligible that cycle. The balance carries forward — but doesn't guarantee coverage.

✗ Dollar Bank is not a flat premium

A flat monthly premium is group billing — fixed cost, continuous coverage. Dollar Bank contributions vary with hours worked: the employer pays a rate per hour (e.g. $2.30), so a busy month means more dollars in the bank. It's still month-to-month, still has a threshold, and still carries forward unused balances — just like Hour Bank, but counted in dollars.

Term 4 of 5
Reciprocity
An electrical worker belongs to Local 402 in Vancouver. This month, her employer sends her to a job site in Calgary — Local 687 territory. The employer submits all her hours to Local 402, but the work happened under Local 687's jurisdiction.

What does reciprocity do here?

✓ Correct — contributions follow the member

Reciprocity is an agreement between union locals that allows contributions to be transferred between plans when a member works across jurisdictions. Local 402 collects the hours and contributions — then sends the relevant portion to Local 687, where the work actually happened.

Without reciprocity, a member could lose contributions made to the wrong plan. Multiplan processes this automatically — no manual transfer, no action needed from the member. It's also why a single employer may submit hours to one local on behalf of workers who actually belong to several.

✗ The transfer is automatic — the worker doesn't choose

Reciprocity is an agreement between locals, not a member-facing choice. The collecting local receives the contribution and transfers the relevant portion automatically. The member doesn't need to do anything — and Multiplan handles the processing without manual intervention.

✗ Reciprocity transfers — it doesn't merge plans

Local 402 and Local 687 remain separate plans with their own rules, rates, and eligibility calculations. Reciprocity creates a mechanism for moving contributions between them when a member works across locals — not combining the plans into one.

Term 5 of 5
Pension (DB / DC)
Two union members are approaching retirement. Member A knows exactly what she'll receive — her pension is calculated from her years of service and final salary, set by the employer. Member B isn't sure of the exact amount — it depends on what he and his employers contributed over the years, and how those contributions have grown.

Member A is on a Defined Benefit plan. Member B is on a Defined Contribution plan. Which statement is accurate?

✓ Correct — the difference is where the certainty sits

Defined Benefit (DB): the retirement payout is defined in advance — typically based on years of service and final salary. The employer bears the investment risk. Member A knows what she'll get.

Defined Contribution (DC): the contribution going in is defined — but the final retirement amount depends on what was contributed and how it performed. Member B accumulated a pot; the value at retirement is the outcome.

Both types are tracked in Multiplan, often for the same union members. A retroactive rate change on a DB plan can require recalculating contributions going back decades — which is exactly why Multiplan stores and processes historical data from the 1960s and 70s.

✗ The distinction isn't the contribution rate

The difference between DB and DC is about where certainty sits — not the rate. In a DB plan, the retirement payout is defined in advance (from service and salary). In a DC plan, the contribution going in is defined — but the final retirement value depends on what accumulated over time. Member A knows what she'll receive; Member B knows what went in, not what will come out.

✗ Those descriptions are reversed

It's the other way around. DB (Defined Benefit) gives a guaranteed payout — Member A knows her retirement income because it's calculated from service and salary. DC (Defined Contribution) accumulates a pot from contributions — Member B doesn't know the final amount until retirement, because it depends on what went in and when.
