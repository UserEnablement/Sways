Scenario 1 of 3

Scoping a new client

The situation

A prospect gets in touch. They're a regional advisory firm managing group benefits for 18 employer clients — a mix of professional services and healthcare businesses. All 18 use standard group billing: core benefits, some optional dental and vision, monthly invoicing to employers. No pension. No hour bank. No union involvement. They want one platform to run all their administration.

Which Oliver product is the right fit — and what's the reasoning that gets you there?

✓ Correct — and the reasoning is what matters here

Oliver Admin is right. The trigger for MultiPlan is never employer count — it's plan type. This firm has 18 employers, but all 18 use straightforward group billing with core and optional benefits. That's exactly what Oliver Admin is built for: group billing with core and optional (voluntary/individual) benefits, whether the client is a single employer or a group of associations.

MultiPlan exists for clients that need multiple plan types in the same arrangement — pension, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, other funds. None of those are present here.

Why this matters in scoping

Putting this client in MultiPlan brings them all the complexity of union trust fund administration they don't need. The scoping error runs the other way too — a union trust fund with contributing employers belongs in MultiPlan, not Admin. The question is always: what plan types does the client need?

✗ The employer count is a red herring

The correct answer is Oliver Admin. Having 18 employers doesn't make a client a MultiPlan candidate. The trigger for MultiPlan is plan type complexity — specifically, clients who need multiple plan types in the same plan: pension, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, union funds. None of those apply here.

This firm uses straightforward group billing with core and optional benefits across all 18 employers. That's an Admin environment, whether it's 1 employer or 100.

The rule to remember

Oliver Admin handles group billing with core and optional (voluntary/individual) benefits — for a single employer or groups and associations with multiple employers. The question is never how many employers. It's always what plan types they need.

✗ Volume doesn't drive the product decision

The correct answer is Oliver Admin. Membership volume and employer count don't determine the product. The deciding factor is plan type: does the client need pension administration, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, or union trust fund management? If none of those apply, this is an Admin environment regardless of scale.

The rule to remember

The question is never how many employers — it is always what plan types does the client need. Group billing with core and optional benefits → Oliver Admin. Multiple plan types together → Oliver MultiPlan.

Scenario 2 of 3

An implementation question

The situation

You're onboarding a new Oliver Admin client — a TPA managing benefits for 12 employers. Implementation is almost complete. A colleague on the project says: "We need to set up the member portal as part of the Admin setup before go-live." They're treating the portal as a step within the Oliver Admin implementation.

Is your colleague correct — and what would you say to them?

✓ Correct — and this is one of the most common internal mix-ups

The Oliver Portal is a separate product with its own contract. Oliver Admin is the back-end system of record — the platform where billing rules live, eligibility is managed, and every transaction is logged. The member and employer portals are a separate CX layer that sits on top, and they require a separate agreement.

This distinction matters at implementation: clients can have Oliver Admin without the portal, and clients can have the portal without certain Admin features. They're bundled as a package deal in many cases — but they're not the same thing, and one doesn't come automatically with the other.

Why this matters in your role

Assuming the portal is included in Admin is one of the most common sources of scope misalignment on new implementations. If a client expects a member-facing portal and it isn't scoped, that's a gap that surfaces at go-live — the worst possible moment.

✗ The portals are not part of Oliver Admin

The correct answer is C. The Oliver Portal (CX layer) is a separate product with its own contract. Oliver Admin is the back-end administration platform — where billing rules are configured, eligibility is tracked, and invoices are produced. The member and employer portals are a separate experience layer. They can be sold together, but one doesn't activate automatically with the other.

Why this matters

This distinction needs to be clear before scoping starts. A client who expects portal access but hasn't contracted for it will discover the gap at exactly the wrong time — usually during go-live or when a member tries to log in.

✗ Neither portal is included automatically

The correct answer is C. Both the member and employer portals are part of the Oliver Portal (CX layer) — a separate product, separate contract, separate scoping conversation. Oliver Admin is the back-end system of record. The portal sits on top of it but is not part of it.

This isn't about which portal comes first — it's that neither is bundled into Oliver Admin by default. Any portal access needs to be identified, scoped, and contracted separately.

Scenario 3 of 3

A support call — existing client

The situation

A TPA administrator calls your support team. One of their employer groups is disputing an invoice from two months ago. The employer says a member was terminated on 15 March but was still charged for the full month of March and all of April. The employer wants a credit and wants to see proof of what happened. The TPA's own records are unclear — the administrator who processed the termination has left the company.

What should your first response be — and which part of Oliver Admin is relevant?

✓ Correct — the audit trail answers questions the TPA's own records can't

This is exactly what the audit trail is for. Every action in Oliver Admin — every eligibility update, every termination, every invoice — is logged with a timestamp and the identity of whoever made the change. The fact that the administrator has left the company doesn't matter. The record is in the system.

In this case, the audit trail will show: when the termination was entered into Oliver Admin, who entered it, and what the eligibility record showed before and after. If the termination was entered late — after the billing cycle had already run — that's visible. If it was entered on time and billing still ran incorrectly, that's visible too. Either way, Oliver Admin has the answer.

Why this matters in support

Knowing that the audit trail exists — and that it's timestamped, complete, and always available — means you can respond to a call like this with confidence rather than uncertainty. You don't need the TPA's internal records. You don't need the person who made the change. You need Oliver Admin.

✗ Oliver Admin already has the answer — you don't need the employer's records

The correct answer is C. Oliver Admin doesn't depend on what happened outside the system — it records what happened inside it. Every termination entered into Oliver Admin is logged with a timestamp and user identity. The question isn't "when did the employer notify the TPA?" — it's "when was the termination entered into Oliver Admin, and did billing run before or after that?"

The audit trail answers that question directly. You don't need external records. Oliver Admin is the system of record.

✗ Rerunning invoices doesn't explain what happened

The correct answer is C. Rerunning invoices might show a difference in amount, but it won't explain why the member was charged or when the termination was recorded. The audit trail does.

The audit trail gives you a timestamped history of every action taken on that member's record: when the termination was entered, by whom, and what eligibility showed before and after. That's the information the employer needs, the TPA needs, and an auditor would need. It's already in the system — you don't need to reconstruct it.
