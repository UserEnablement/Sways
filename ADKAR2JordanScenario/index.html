ADKAR · Branching Scenario
Meet Jordan

Same employee.
Different signals.

Jordan has always been a strong performer. Reliable. Experienced. Trusted by clients.

Since the new process launched, things feel… uneven.

What you're observing
✅ Some days: on board ❌ Other days: frustrated ⚠️ Sometimes: disengaged ✅ Still completing tasks ❌ Missing deadlines ⚠️ Taking longer than before

As a leader, what do you do next? Do you push harder? Send Jordan back to training? Assume it's resistance?

Or do you pause long enough to figure out what's really missing?

This scenario will help you practice diagnosing before acting. At each step, you'll choose how to respond — and see what happens next.

💡 Your objective

Your goal is not to fix Jordan quickly. Your goal is to diagnose accurately — then act with intent.

Decision Point 1 of 2

Jordan misses another deadline.

The situation

It's the third week since the new process launched. Jordan has submitted two reports with errors and missed a client deadline. In your check-in, Jordan is quiet — gives short answers, avoids eye contact. When you ask how it's going, Jordan says: "Fine. I'm just figuring it out."

You've seen Jordan perform at a high level for years. Something is off — but you're not sure what.

Your decision

What do you do first?

What happened next

You had the conversation.

⚠️ Two weeks later

Jordan listened, nodded, and thanked you for the clarity. Performance improved briefly — then slipped back. The errors are still there. The deadlines are still being missed. Jordan seems more anxious now, not less.

The conversation felt productive. But nothing changed.

🔍 What this tells us

You treated this as a Desire gap — assuming Jordan didn't want to comply. But Jordan was already trying. Reminding someone of expectations doesn't help when the real barrier is somewhere else.


Before acting, the question is always: what is actually missing? Awareness? Desire? Knowledge? Ability? Reinforcement?

💡 ADKAR insight

Motivation-based interventions only work when Desire is genuinely the gap. Applied to the wrong barrier, they can increase anxiety and erode trust.

Let's go back and try a different approach.

What happened next

You asked. Jordan opened up — slightly.

✅ The conversation

Jordan paused, then said: "I know what we're supposed to do. I just… it takes me so much longer than before. And I don't want to look like I can't handle it."

This is new information. Jordan understands the change. Jordan wants to succeed. But something is still getting in the way.

💡 What you're hearing

Jordan said "I know what we're supposed to do." That rules out an Awareness or Knowledge gap. Jordan also clearly cares about performing well — that rules out a Desire gap. So what's missing?

Decision Point 2 — Your response

Jordan knows what to do but is struggling to execute consistently. What do you do next?

What happened next

Jordan repeated the training.

⚠️ One week later

Jordan completed the training module and scored 88% on the knowledge check. But in the next client report, the same errors appeared. Jordan told a colleague: "I don't understand what they want from me. I passed the test."

The training worked. The performance didn't improve. These are two different things.

🔍 What this tells us

You treated this as a Knowledge gap — assuming Jordan didn't know what to do. But Jordan passed the training the first time too. Knowing what to do and being able to do it consistently under real conditions are not the same thing.


In ADKAR, Knowledge is understanding how to change. Ability is performing it in real working conditions. They look similar from the outside — but they require very different responses.

💡 ADKAR insight

Training only addresses Knowledge. If the gap is Ability, more training won't close it — coaching, practice time, and psychological safety will.

Let's go back and look more carefully at what Jordan is actually experiencing.

What happened next

You adjusted Jordan's targets.

⚠️ Three weeks later

Jordan felt relieved — and grateful. But the reduced targets became the new normal. Other team members noticed. Two of them asked why Jordan's workload was lighter. The underlying gap — the actual reason for Jordan's struggles — was never addressed.

You removed the pressure. But you didn't remove the barrier.

🔍 What this tells us

Accommodation can be a compassionate short-term move — but when it replaces diagnosis, it becomes avoidance. Jordan needed support to build capability, not a reduced expectation to perform around the gap.


The ADKAR barrier was still there. The consequences were just deferred.

💡 ADKAR insight

An Ability gap requires coaching, practice, and time — not reduced standards. Lowering the bar doesn't build capability; it hides the gap until it becomes a bigger problem.

What happened next

You named it — and acted on it.

✅ What you said

"Jordan, what I'm hearing is that you understand the process and you want to get this right — you're just not there yet in terms of speed and consistency under pressure. That's an Ability gap, and it's normal at this stage. Let's work on it together."

You blocked 30 minutes twice a week for Jordan to practice the new process with you available for questions. You also removed one lower-priority task temporarily to create space.

✅ Three weeks later

Jordan's confidence visibly improved. Errors dropped by more than half. Jordan told you: "I think I've finally got it. I just needed time to actually do it, not just learn it."

💪 Ability gap — correctly diagnosed and addressed
💡 Why this worked

You listened before acting. You named the gap specifically — not "resistance," not "a training issue," but an Ability gap. Then you gave Jordan what an Ability gap actually needs: coaching, practice time, and psychological safety to struggle without consequence.

What happened next

You increased your check-ins.

⚠️ Two weeks later

Jordan started preparing extensively before every check-in — spending time managing your perception rather than developing the skill. Performance under observation improved slightly. Performance when you weren't watching stayed inconsistent. Jordan seemed more stressed than before.

More oversight created more anxiety — not more capability.

🔍 What this tells us

Oversight is a Reinforcement tool — it's useful when people have the ability but need accountability to sustain new behaviors. Applied to an Ability gap, it creates pressure without support. Jordan needed coaching, not monitoring.


In ADKAR, using the right tool for the wrong gap doesn't just fail — it can actively make things worse.

💡 ADKAR insight

Accountability without capability is just pressure. Coaching is different from monitoring — one builds the person, the other measures them.

Debrief

What Jordan's story reveals.

Jordan wasn't resistant. Jordan wasn't undertrained. Jordan had an Ability gap — the gap between knowing what to do and being able to do it consistently under real conditions.

The signals were mixed because Ability gaps often look like Desire gaps from the outside. Frustration, avoidance, and inconsistency can mean many things. That's why diagnosis comes before action.

💡 The key principle

Every ADKAR element requires a different response. Training fixes Knowledge. Conversations fix Desire. Coaching fixes Ability. Accountability fixes Reinforcement. Applying the wrong fix — even a good one — to the wrong gap is where change efforts break down.
