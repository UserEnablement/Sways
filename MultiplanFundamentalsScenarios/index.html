Scenario 1 of 3
New client conversation

A prospect gets in touch. They manage benefits for 22 employers — a mix of retail and hospitality businesses. All 22 use straightforward group billing: core benefits, some optional dental and vision add-ons, monthly invoicing. No pension. No hour bank. No union involvement. They want a single platform to manage all of them.

Which product do you recommend — and what's the reasoning that gets you there?

✓ Correct — and the reasoning is what matters here

Oliver Admin is right. The trigger for Multiplan is never employer count — it's plan type complexity. This prospect has 22 employers, but all 22 are using straightforward group billing with core and optional benefits. That's exactly what Oliver Admin is built for.

Multiplan exists for clients that have multiple plan types in the same plan — pension, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, other funds. None of those are present here.

Why this matters in scoping

Putting this client in Multiplan would bring them all the complexity of union trust fund administration they don't need. The scoping error runs the other way too — a union trust fund with 22 contributing employers belongs in Multiplan, not Admin. The question is always: what plan types does the client need?

✗ Not quite — the employer count is a red herring

The correct answer is Oliver Admin. Having 22 employers doesn't make a client a Multiplan candidate. The trigger for Multiplan is plan type complexity — clients who need multiple plan types in the same plan: pension, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, union funds.

This prospect uses straightforward group billing with core and optional benefits across all 22 employers. That's an Admin environment, whether it's 1 employer or 100.

The rule to remember

Oliver Admin handles group billing with core and optional benefits — for a single employer or groups and associations with multiple employers. The question is never how many employers. It's always what plan types they need.

✗ Volume doesn't drive the product decision

The correct answer is Oliver Admin. Membership volume and employer count don't determine the product. The deciding factor is plan type: does the client need pension administration, hour-bank or dollar-bank eligibility, or union trust fund management? If none of those apply, this is an Admin environment regardless of scale.

The rule to remember

Group billing with core and optional benefits → Oliver Admin. Multiple plan types together (pension, hour bank, dollar bank) → Oliver Multiplan. Scale is irrelevant to this decision.

Scenario 2 of 3
Support call — existing Multiplan client

A member services administrator calls. One of their members — a construction worker — had a quiet month in February. His hours fell below the plan's threshold. He's expecting to be covered in April but the report shows him as not eligible. The administrator wants to know: can he stay covered? He's willing to pay himself. The plan also has a hardship provision.

What's the right response — and which part of Multiplan is relevant here?

✓ Correct — two features, not one

Self-pay and subsidy management are both relevant here. Self-pay allows a member whose hour bank has run dry to pay the premium themselves and maintain eligibility. Whether that's available depends on the plan rules.

The hardship provision is the subsidy function: if the member can't cover the full amount themselves, the plan can apply a subsidy to make up the shortfall. Plan-dependent — but this is exactly the feature that exists for situations like this.

Why this matters in support

Knowing these two features exist — and that they're separate from hour-bank eligibility — means you can give a useful first response on a call like this, rather than saying "I'll need to check."

✗ There's more available than this

The correct answer is C. Hour-bank eligibility determines coverage based on hours worked — but when a member's bank runs dry, that's not necessarily the end. Oliver Multiplan has two features specifically designed for this situation.

Self-pay lets a member maintain eligibility by paying the premium themselves. Subsidy management allows the plan to cover any shortfall — relevant here because the plan has a hardship provision.

The distinction to hold onto

Hour-bank eligibility is the standard route to coverage. Self-pay and subsidy management are what Multiplan offers when that standard route is unavailable. They're sequential, not the same thing.

✗ Carry-forward works differently to this

The correct answer is C. Hour banks do carry forward — surplus hours from good months go into the bank for future eligibility. But in this scenario, the member's bank has already run dry. There are no banked hours left to draw on. Carry-forward doesn't generate coverage from nothing.

What's available when the bank is empty is self-pay and subsidy management. Both are part of Multiplan — and both are relevant here.

The distinction to hold onto

Carry-forward is how banked hours accumulate over good months. Self-pay and subsidy management are what happens when the bank is exhausted. Different mechanisms, different moments.

Scenario 3 of 3
Internal conversation — new colleague

A new joiner asks you: "I get that Multiplan is complex — but why do clients stay? Couldn't they move to a competitor if they were unhappy?" You want to give them an honest, useful answer — not just a sales line.

Which answer is most accurate — and most useful for a new joiner to understand?

✓ Correct — and this is worth understanding deeply

Data depth is the real answer. A union trust fund on Multiplan for 20 years has 20 years of pension contribution history, eligibility records, retroactive calculations, and audit trails inside the platform. Moving that data to a competitor — accurately, traceably, legally — is an enormous undertaking. Most clients conclude it's not worth it.

This is what's meant when Multiplan is described as Oliver's "deepest competitive moat." It's not primarily about features. It's about the accumulation of irreplaceable, hard-to-migrate data over decades of use.

The revenue connection

Clients don't just stay because they're happy — they stay because leaving is structurally difficult. That creates predictable, durable recurring revenue for Oliver. Every implementation done well today compounds in value over years, not just months.

✗ Feature quality is part of the story, but not the core one

The correct answer is B. Multiplan does handle things that competitors rarely match. But the primary reason clients stay isn't feature comparison — it's switching cost.

A client with 30 years of pension contribution history faces an enormous task if they want to move: migrating decades of legally accountable data, accurately, to a new system. Most decide that's not worth attempting — regardless of what a competitor offers.

Why this framing matters

Understanding retention as a product of data depth means you understand why Multiplan implementations need to be done well. Every year a client stays, the switching cost grows. Getting it right at implementation creates compounding loyalty.

✗ Contracts matter, but they're not the real answer

The correct answer is B. Contract terms shape pricing and commitment — but contract length isn't what keeps Multiplan clients in place. Data is.

A union trust fund with decades of pension history, eligibility records, and audit trails faces a structural challenge in moving: that data has to go somewhere, and migrating it accurately is genuinely difficult. That stickiness is far more durable than any contractual term.

The honest version

Clients stay because switching means taking on a high-risk, high-cost data migration for no guaranteed improvement. The SaaS model means Oliver earns recurring revenue as long as that data lives in the platform — which is usually a very long time.
