Scenario 1 of 3
New client conversation

A broker contacts you. They manage group benefits for 18 employer groups. They're spending most of their team's time on remittances, billing, carrier coordination, and month-end reporting — all manual, all painful. They want to keep talking to their employer clients directly. But they've been clear: they don't want a new software system to manage. They want someone to take the work away and run it for them.

What do you recommend — and what's the reasoning?

✓ Correct — and the reasoning matters as much as the answer

OLLIE back-office model is right. The broker has given you two clear signals. First: they don't want to manage a system — that rules out the Oliver platform, which requires the client to run their own administration. Second: they want to keep talking to their employer clients directly — that rules out full-service, which hands over the front end too.

Back-office is built for exactly this: OLLIE takes the operational complexity — remittances, billing, carrier coordination, reporting — while the broker stays the face to their clients. The client is the face. OLLIE is the engine.

The distinction to hold onto

The question is never just "do they want Oliver?" It's always "what do they want Oliver to do for them?" When the answer is "do the work" — that's OLLIE. When the answer is "give us better tools" — that's the Oliver platform.

✗ Not quite — the broker was explicit about what they don't want

The correct answer is OLLIE back-office model. The Oliver platform gives organizations better technology — but the team still has to use it to run administration themselves. This broker said clearly: they don't want a new system to manage. They want the work taken away.

That's the fundamental distinction. Platform = technology to run yourself. OLLIE = administration run for you.

The rule to remember

If they want tools — Oliver platform. If they want the work done — OLLIE. The broker's exact words are the clearest possible signal of which conversation this is.

✗ Not quite — the broker wants to keep the client relationship

The correct answer is OLLIE back-office model. Full-service hands over everything — including member and employer contact. But this broker was clear: they want to keep talking to their employer clients directly. That front-end relationship is the one thing they're not willing to outsource.

Back-office is designed for exactly this: OLLIE runs the engine, the broker keeps the relationship.

The signal to watch for

"We want to keep talking to our clients" = back-office. "We want everything handled" = full-service. The presence or absence of that sentence decides the model in almost every OLLIE scoping conversation.

Scenario 2 of 3
Internal scoping meeting

You're in a scoping meeting. A new client has just signed on to OLLIE. Your colleague says: "I've gone ahead and booked the same onboarding flow we use for all our Oliver Admin clients — same steps, same timeline, same technical setup. OLLIE runs on the Oliver platform anyway, so the process should be identical."

Is your colleague right — and how would you respond?

✓ Correct — this distinction matters from day one of the engagement

OLLIE is a managed service engagement — not a platform onboarding. Yes, OLLIE runs on the Oliver platform. But what's being configured isn't just technology — it's an operating model. That means eligibility workflows, remittance processing rules, billing configurations, carrier coordination, reporting, and resourcing. The client isn't learning to use a system — they're handing over their operations.

Using an Oliver Admin onboarding flow misses all of that. It treats the client like they're self-administering, when they've specifically chosen not to.

Why this matters in your work

Getting the onboarding model wrong at the start creates rework, resourcing confusion, and a client relationship that starts on the wrong foot. OLLIE clients expect OLLIE to own the outcome — that has to be scoped and set up correctly from day one.

✗ The shared platform doesn't mean a shared process

The correct answer is B. It's true that OLLIE runs on the Oliver platform — but what's being onboarded is fundamentally different. An Oliver Admin onboarding sets up a client who will run their own administration. An OLLIE onboarding sets up a managed service: eligibility workflows, remittance processing, billing rules, carrier coordination, and operational resourcing — all configured for a client handing their operations over to OLLIE.

Same technology. Completely different setup.

The distinction to hold onto

OLLIE is a service. Oliver Admin is a product. They share a platform — but onboarding a service means configuring operations, not just technology. That difference shows up immediately in scoping, resourcing, and timeline.

✗ It's not the same process — it's a different type of engagement entirely

The correct answer is B. The difference isn't just timeline length — it's what's being set up. An Oliver Admin onboarding configures technology for a client who will self-administer. An OLLIE onboarding configures an operating model: eligibility workflows, billing rules, carrier coordination, processing logic, and the people and responsibilities behind it.

Adding more time to the same process doesn't fix the problem. You need a different process designed for a managed service engagement.

Why this matters in your work

If you're in Implementation & Delivery or Support & Client Success, this is one of the most important things to get right early. Scoping the wrong engagement type means the client doesn't receive what they signed up for.

Scenario 3 of 3
New joiner terminology question

A new colleague pulls you aside after a team meeting. "I keep hearing 'OLLIE' and 'Oliver' in the same conversations — sometimes by the same person in the same sentence. Are they just different names for the same thing? And what's PPA? Someone mentioned it and nobody explained it."

What's the most accurate way to explain all three?

✓ Correct — and this is the most common confusion in OLLIE conversations

Three distinct things, used precisely. Oliver is the platform and the broader company — in the benefits and pension administration space for more than 40 years. OLLIE is OLLIE Insurance Solutions, the administration service that uses the Oliver platform to manage benefits, pension, billing, and insurance programs for clients who want the work run for them.

PPA — Platform-Powered Administration — is Oliver's deliberate positioning term. It signals that OLLIE isn't a traditional manual TPA: it's administration delivered through modern technology, at scale, with better outcomes. When someone in the industry hears "TPA," they often think inflexible and legacy. PPA is the reframe.

The rule that makes it stick

Oliver = platform and company. OLLIE = the administration service. PPA = what OLLIE delivers. They are related — but not interchangeable. Using them precisely is the difference between a conversation that lands and one that creates confusion.

✗ OLLIE is not a nickname — it's a distinct service with its own identity

The correct answer is C. OLLIE isn't informal or interchangeable with Oliver. OLLIE Insurance Solutions is a distinct service offering — the administration business that Oliver operates for clients who want their back-office work managed for them. Oliver is the platform and company. OLLIE is what runs on it, as a service.

And PPA isn't a technical architecture term. It's Oliver's deliberate positioning language: a signal to the market that this is modern, technology-enabled administration — not legacy TPA work.

Why precision matters

When Oliver and OLLIE get used interchangeably, clients and colleagues lose track of what's being offered. A client asking about "Oliver" may mean the platform. One asking about "OLLIE" means the service. The distinction changes the entire conversation.

✗ OLLIE is newer than Oliver — not the other way around

The correct answer is C. Oliver is the original — the platform and company in the benefits and pension space for more than 40 years. OLLIE is the newer offering: the administration service built on the Oliver platform. Neither is being phased out — they serve different purposes in the same ecosystem.

And PPA — Platform-Powered Administration — isn't a pricing term. It's Oliver's name for what OLLIE delivers: the combination of modern platform technology and expert administration service.

The order to remember

Oliver came first — the platform. OLLIE came next — the service. PPA is the positioning term that describes what happens when you combine them. Three things, each with a specific meaning.
