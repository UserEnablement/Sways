Term 1 of 6
System of Record
A member disputes whether they were covered during March. The employer says yes. The carrier says no. The TPA opens Oliver Admin and checks the eligibility record. Whatever Oliver Admin shows is the answer the auditor accepts.

In this situation, Oliver Admin is acting as the system of record. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — and this is one of the most important things to understand about Admin

System of record means authoritative. Not the most recent version, not one view among many — the definitive source. When Oliver Admin records that a member is eligible, that record is what billing, carrier files, and audits are built from.

This is why every change in Oliver Admin is logged, timestamped, and traceable. If the system of record is wrong, everything built on top of it is wrong. If it's right, everything flows from a reliable foundation.

✗ Not a backup — the primary source

A backup stores copies of data from elsewhere. Oliver Admin is not a backup — it is the primary source. Other systems (employer portals, carrier files, reports) draw from what Admin holds. When there's a dispute, Admin isn't consulted as a secondary check. It's consulted because it is the answer.

✗ Not a reporting layer — the source itself

Reporting tools summarise what other systems hold. Oliver Admin is what they summarise. It doesn't reflect what happened elsewhere — it records what happened, and those records are authoritative. The reports, invoices, and carrier files are all outputs from the system of record, not the record itself.

Term 2 of 6
TPA (Third-Party Administrator)
A company wants to offer health benefits to its 200 employees. Rather than managing enrolments, invoices, and carrier files themselves, they hire an external organisation to handle all of it. That organisation uses Oliver Admin as their central platform to run the administration for this company — and 14 others like it.

The organisation running the benefits administration is a TPA. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — and TPAs are Oliver Admin's most common client type

TPAs sit between employers and insurance carriers. Employers fund the benefits. Carriers underwrite and pay the claims. The TPA manages everything in between: enrolments, eligibility, invoicing, carrier files, reconciliation, and member service.

A TPA using Oliver Admin will typically manage benefits for multiple employer groups — sometimes dozens — all through a single platform. That's precisely what Oliver Admin is built for.

✗ Carriers underwrite — TPAs administer

An insurance carrier is the company that underwrites risk and pays out claims — Canada Life, Manulife, Claim Secure. A TPA is not a carrier. The TPA manages the administrative layer: who is enrolled, what they're covered for, what the employer owes, and how that data flows to the carrier. Oliver Admin is the platform the TPA uses to do that work.

✗ Regulators set rules — TPAs operate within them

Government regulators set the rules. TPAs operate within them — running the day-to-day administration of group benefits on behalf of employers. Oliver Admin helps TPAs do that work accurately, with the audit trail and compliance architecture regulators expect to see.

Term 3 of 6
Group Billing
At the end of each month, a TPA running Oliver Admin needs to tell 15 employer groups what they owe for their employees' benefits coverage. Each employer has different plan designs, contribution rules, and member counts. The amounts change when members join, leave, or change their coverage. Before Oliver Admin, two people spent a full day doing this manually, every month.

The monthly process of calculating what each employer owes and generating invoices is called group billing. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — this is the core monthly cycle Oliver Admin automates

Group billing is the heartbeat of benefits administration. Every month: premiums are calculated, invoices are generated, and employer payments are reconciled. Oliver Admin runs this cycle automatically — rules configured once, invoices produced on demand.

It's also where errors are most costly. A billing mistake doesn't just affect one line — it cascades across employers, members, and carriers. This is why accurate group billing is central to what Oliver Admin is trusted to do.

✗ That's remittance reconciliation — group billing comes first

Reconciling payments after they arrive is part of remittance processing — a separate step that comes after billing. Group billing is what happens first: calculating what each employer owes and generating the invoice. You can't reconcile a payment until the invoice has been produced.

✗ That's carrier file production — a different output

Sending eligibility data to insurance carriers is carrier file production — a separate process. Group billing is about employer invoices: calculating premiums, generating statements, and reconciling what's owed. Both are outputs Oliver Admin produces, but they serve different purposes and flow in different directions.

Term 4 of 6
Eligibility
An employee gets married in April and adds their spouse to their plan. Their coverage category changes from individual to couple. Their premium changes. In May, their employer switches them to a different benefit class. Oliver Admin tracks all of this automatically — updating who is covered, for which benefits, from which date.

Oliver Admin is managing the member's eligibility. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — and eligibility drives everything else

Eligibility is the foundation. Before a premium can be calculated, an invoice generated, or a carrier file produced, Oliver Admin needs to know: is this member covered? For what? From when?

Eligibility rules are configured in the platform once — then applied automatically whenever a life event occurs: a new hire, a marriage, a benefit class change, a termination. The invoice knows. The carrier file knows. The audit trail records it. Eligibility doesn't just feed billing — it feeds everything.

✗ That's the premium calculation — eligibility comes first

The employer's monthly cost is the premium — part of group billing. But premiums can only be calculated once eligibility is established. Eligibility answers who is covered and for what. Once that's confirmed, the premium follows. Eligibility is the input; the invoice amount is the output.

✗ That's remittance reconciliation — not eligibility

Matching employer payments against carrier approvals is remittance processing — a later step in the billing cycle. Eligibility is the earlier question: is this member actually covered? That question is answered by rules configured in Oliver Admin, applied at the member level, and updated whenever coverage changes.

Term 5 of 6
Remittance
An employer receives their monthly invoice from the TPA: $42,000 for their 80 enrolled employees. They transfer the funds. On the carrier side, Manulife has approved $41,200 worth of coverage for that month — a $800 difference due to a termination processed late. The TPA needs to reconcile these two numbers and ensure the carrier is paid correctly.

The employer's payment to the plan, reconciled against what the carrier approves, is the remittance process. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — remittance is about money in and accuracy confirmed

Remittance closes the billing loop. The invoice goes out. The employer pays. Oliver Admin reconciles what was billed against what the carrier approved — flagging any differences and creating the audit trail.

Discrepancies happen. A late termination, a missed life event, a new hire whose paperwork arrived after billing ran. Remittance processing catches those gaps and ensures the carrier receives what they should — no more, no less.

✗ That's the invoice — remittance is what happens after

The invoice is generated during group billing — it's what the employer receives telling them what they owe. Remittance is what happens next: the employer pays, and Oliver Admin reconciles that payment against what the carrier has approved. Invoice first, remittance after.

✗ That's carrier file production — a different output

The eligibility file sent to carriers is carrier file production — data out to the insurer confirming member coverage. Remittance is money in from the employer, reconciled against what the carrier has approved. Both involve the carrier, but in different directions and for different purposes.

Term 6 of 6
Audit Trail
An employer disputes an invoice from three months ago. They say a termination was processed too late, and they were charged for a member who had already left. The TPA opens Oliver Admin and finds the exact date the termination was entered, who entered it, and what the coverage showed before and after. The auditor accepts this as documentation.

Oliver Admin's audit trail made this possible. Which definition fits?

✓ Correct — and this is what 'system of record' means in practice

The audit trail is automatic and always on. Every change in Oliver Admin — every eligibility update, every invoice, every correction — is logged with a timestamp and the identity of who made the change. It doesn't need to be created. It's already there.

This is what makes Oliver Admin a reliable system of record rather than just a database. When a dispute arises, an auditor asks a question, or a client needs documentation — the answer is already in the system. No reconstruction. No memory. Just the record.

✗ Not a report — always-on history

A monthly pre-billing report is something administrators run on demand. The audit trail is not run — it just exists. Every change, every transaction, every correction is logged automatically as it happens. You don't generate the audit trail. You look it up. And it's always there, going back as far as the data in the system.

✗ Not manual — automatic and continuous

Manual checklists depend on someone remembering to complete them. Oliver Admin's audit trail is automatic. Every action in the system is recorded — timestamp, user, and what changed — without any administrator effort. This is part of what makes it compliant by design, not by intention.
